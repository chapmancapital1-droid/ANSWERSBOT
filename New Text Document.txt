as my senior application develer help me build a full stack application design for this ideaHere's a full-stack architecture for Answerspot — an AI answer-monitoring platform for local service businesses.
Core Concept
Track what AI platforms (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews) say when users query for local services, so businesses can see if/how they appear and fix their visibility.

System Architecture
Frontend: Next.js 14 (App Router) + TypeScript + Tailwind + shadcn/ui
Backend: Node.js/NestJS API + Python workers (for LLM querying/parsing)
Database: PostgreSQL (core data) + Redis (queues/cache)
Queue: BullMQ or Temporal for scheduled scans
Auth: Clerk or Auth.js
Payments: Stripe (subscription tiers)
Hosting: Vercel (frontend), Railway/Render/AWS ECS (backend + workers)

Data Model (Postgres)
organizations       (id, name, plan, stripe_customer_id)
users               (id, org_id, email, role)
businesses          (id, org_id, name, category, city, state, website)
tracked_queries     (id, business_id, query_text, location)   e.g. "best roofer in Austin"
platforms           (id, name)                                 ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini...
scans               (id, tracked_query_id, platform_id, run_at, status)
scan_results        (id, scan_id, raw_response, mentioned bool,
                     rank_position, sentiment, citations jsonb, competitors jsonb)
recommendations     (id, business_id, type, severity, message, resolved)

Key Services
1. Query Runner (Python workers)
Hits each AI platform's API (or headless-browser fallback) with the tracked queries on a schedule (daily/weekly per plan tier).
2. Response Parser
Uses an LLM to extract structured data from raw AI answers: was the business mentioned? at what position? which competitors appeared? what citations were used? sentiment?
3. Recommendation Engine
Compares your presence vs competitors, checks schema markup / citations / review signals, and generates actionable fixes.

Core API Endpoints
POST   /businesses
GET    /businesses/:id/visibility-score
POST   /businesses/:id/queries
GET    /queries/:id/results?platform=&range=
POST   /scans/trigger
GET    /businesses/:id/competitors
GET    /businesses/:id/recommendations
POST   /webhooks/stripe

MVP Build Order

Manual query tracking + one platform (Perplexity API is easiest)
Scheduled scan worker + result storage
Parser to detect mention/rank/competitors
Dashboard (visibility score, trend chart, competitor table)

Who It's For & The Core Job
The customer is a non-technical local operator — a roofer, dentist, HVAC contractor, auto shop owner. They don't know what "schema markup" or "citation" means. The product's entire job is to answer three questions in plain language: Am I showing up when customers ask AI for a business like mine? Who's beating me? What exactly do I do about it?
Everything below serves that.
The Feature Set
1. Onboarding that does the work for them
They type their business name and city. The app auto-detects category, pulls their website, Google Business Profile, and existing reviews, and auto-generates the 15–30 queries a real customer would type ("best roofer near me," "emergency roof repair Austin," "who does metal roofing in Round Rock"). No blank forms. They confirm and go. First scan runs immediately so they see value inside two minutes.
2. The Visibility Score (the hero metric)
One number, 0–100, updated after each scan. It rolls up: how often you appear, average rank position when you do, sentiment of how you're described, and citation strength. This is the thing they check obsessively and screenshot to their partner. Trend line over time is the retention hook.
3. Answer Explorer
The literal AI responses, side by side across platforms (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews). Your mentions highlighted, competitors highlighted in another color. Operators need to see the actual words the AI used — that's the "oh wow" moment that makes the abstract concrete.
4. Competitor Radar
Auto-identifies who keeps appearing in your slots. Shows you vs top 3–5 rivals across every query. "Precision Roofing appears in 8 of your 12 queries; you appear in 3." This creates the competitive urgency that drives payment and reduces churn — nobody wants to lose to the shop down the road.
5. Fix-It Recommendations (the money feature)
Monitoring alone is a dashboard people stop opening. The recommendations are what justify a subscription. Each is ranked by impact, written in plain English, and tells them the specific action: "Add these 3 service keywords to your homepage," "You have 40 reviews but no responses — AI weights response rate; reply to these," "Your competitor has FAQ schema and you don't — here's the exact code to send your web person." Where possible, generate the artifact for them (the schema snippet, the review-response draft, the homepage copy).
6. Alerts
Email/SMS when your score drops, a competitor overtakes you, or sentiment turns negative. This pulls them back in without needing to open the app — critical for a tool that scans on a schedule.
7. White-label reporting
Agencies managing many local businesses are your highest-value segment. Branded PDF/link reports they can send clients. This unlocks a whole B2B2C channel.
The Dashboard Structure
Home is the Visibility Score front and center, trend line, and the top 3 recommendations. Below that, Competitor Radar summary and recent answer changes. Secondary tabs: Answer Explorer, Competitors, Recommendations, Queries, Reports, Settings/Billing.
What Actually Makes It Successful (beyond features)
Speed to first insight. If they don't see their own AI answers within minutes of signing up, they leave. The onboarding must front-load the payoff.
Plain language, always. Every technical concept translated. "Citations" becomes "which websites the AI trusted." This is the moat against generic SEO tools that talk over their heads.
Recommendations that produce artifacts, not homework. The difference between $29/mo churn and $99/mo retention is whether you tell them what's wrong or hand them the fix.
Defensible data. Track results over time so you own the historical trend — a new competitor can't replicate months of a customer's scan history. That's the switching cost.
Trust and honesty. The disclaimer in your mockup ("estimates are educational and based on assumptions") matters. AI answers are non-deterministic; over-promising exact rankings will burn trust fast. Frame it as directional intelligence, run multiple samples per query, and show confidence ranges rather than false precision.
Pricing (drives the roadmap)
Starter (~$49/mo): 1 business, weekly scans, core dashboard. Pro (~$99–149): daily scans, all platforms, full recommendations, alerts. Agency (~$299+): multi-location, white-label reports, API. Free trial or a one-time free scan as the acquisition hook — the free scan showing "you appear in only 1 of 12 queries" sells itself.
Build Priority
Nail this narrow loop first: onboard → auto-generate queries → scan one platform → show the actual answers with your score → give three real recommendations. That single loop, done well, is a fundable MVP. Everything else (multi-platform, competitor radar, white-label) layers on after that core "oh wow" is proven.

What you've got now, and why it matters:
packages/shared-types is the single definition of every shape crossing the API/web boundary. Both sides import it, so a change to a Prisma model propagates to one file instead of drifting silently between backend and frontend. This is the thing that prevents the classic "the API returns snake_case but the frontend expects camelCase" bug six months in.
apps/web/lib/api.ts is the one auth/error/caching implementation every page shares. It works in both contexts — reads the session cookie on the server, relies on the httpOnly cookie in the browser — via a dynamic import of next/headers that keeps server-only code out of the client bundle. The typed api.business.get(id) wrappers mean pages never hand-write fetch URLs, and the ApiError class lets any caller branch on .status (401 vs 404 vs 403). Everything else monitors; this is what turns monitoring into action.
The architectural decision I'd point to as the important one: it separates what's true from what to do. signals.py computes plain facts from scan results (appearance rate, which competitor beats you where, which mentions have no sources backing them). rules.py reads only those signals — never the database — so each rule is a pure function you can unit-test with a hand-built BusinessSignals object and zero fixtures. That's what keeps the core value logic testable and lets someone add a new recommendation type by writing one function and adding one list entry, without touching the engine.
The other deliberate choice: the LLM is confined to the edges. Rules are deterministic code — the LLM never decides what fires. It only drafts human-facing copy (review replies), behind a clean interface with a working fallback, so the engine and its tests run without an API key and LLM cost stays near zero. This matters because "let the LLM decide the recommendations" is the tempting-but-wrong version: it's untestable, non-deterministic, expensive, and impossible to reason about when it misfires.
And the three things that make the output usable rather than overwhelming: ranking by impact, dedupe to one per type, and a hard cap of five. An operator acts on a short prioritized list; they ignore a wall of thirty. That cap is a product decision as much as a technical one.
A couple of honest limits. The rules and their impact weights here are a reasonable first set, not a calibrated one — like the score weights in ADR 0005, the real impact values need to be tuned against actual outcome data once you have it, and the thresholds (why 2 citation gaps, why 60% response rate) are judgment calls a domain expert should sanity-check. The engine wiring in the Celery task still has two TODO(M5) markers: building BusinessSignals from the actual scan_results rows, and loading review data from whatever profile source you use — those are the real integration points. And the review-response LLM call is stubbed with a deterministic fallback; when you wire the real one, it needs a tight prompt and guardrails so it doesn't draft something off-brand.
